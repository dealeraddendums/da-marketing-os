Walk through any new car dealer's lot and you'll typically see two documents on every vehicle's window: the federally required Monroney label (the window sticker) and the dealer addendum. Customers often glance at both without understanding why there are two. Your sales staff should be able to explain the difference clearly — and more importantly, your documents need to be accurate and compliant.

Here's what each document is, what it must contain, and why getting either one wrong is a problem.

## The Window Sticker (Monroney Label)

The Monroney label is a federal requirement for all new vehicles sold in the United States. It's named after Senator Almer Monroney, who sponsored the Automobile Information Disclosure Act of 1958.

**What it shows:**
- Base MSRP (manufacturer's suggested retail price)
- Factory-installed options and their individual prices
- Destination and delivery charge
- Fuel economy ratings (MPG)
- Vehicle identification number (VIN)
- Country of origin and parts content information
- Safety and crash test ratings (for vehicles that have been tested)

**Who creates it:** The manufacturer. Your dealership does not create or modify the Monroney label.

**Federal rule:** It is illegal for anyone other than the ultimate purchaser to remove the Monroney label from a new vehicle. This includes your sales staff, your detailers, and your F&I team. The sticker must remain on the vehicle throughout the sale process.

**One important note:** The Monroney label shows *factory* options only. Anything your dealership adds after the vehicle arrives — accessories, protection packages, appearance upgrades — does not appear on the Monroney label. That's where the dealer addendum comes in.

## The Dealer Addendum

The dealer addendum is a separate document that your dealership creates and posts alongside the Monroney label. Unlike the window sticker, you control everything on it — and you're responsible for its accuracy.

**What it typically shows:**
- Dealer-installed accessories (remote start, roof rack, protection films, appearance packages)
- Dealer-added F&I products offered at time of sale (paint protection, tire-and-wheel coverage, GAP, extended warranties)
- Dealer market adjustment or markup above MSRP (if applicable)
- Total of Monroney + dealer additions

**Who creates it:** Your dealership. This is your document.

**Legal requirement:** There is no specific federal law requiring a dealer addendum, but there are strong FTC rules around transparency in pricing. Any dealer-added charges must be clearly disclosed before the sale. The addendum is the standard vehicle for making those disclosures.

**State-specific rules:** Some states have additional requirements about what must appear on the addendum and how it must be formatted. California, for example, has specific rules about how dealer-installed accessories must be described.

## Why Accuracy Matters on Both Documents

### On the Monroney label

You don't control what's on it, but you are responsible for displaying it. If a Monroney label is missing, damaged, or has been removed, the vehicle cannot legally be offered for sale until it's replaced. Manufacturers can provide replacement labels; your factory rep is the starting point.

### On the dealer addendum

You control every line item — and that means you're responsible for every line item. Common addendum problems that create legal exposure:

**Vague line items.** "Dealer preparation: $495" tells a customer nothing. If a state AG or the FTC investigates a consumer complaint, they'll want to know what that $495 paid for. Itemize specifically: "Lot preparation fee — interior detail, exterior wash, fluid check: $495."

**Products listed that weren't installed.** If your addendum says a vehicle has paint protection applied and it doesn't, that's a consumer fraud issue — regardless of whether it was an honest mistake.

**Prices that don't match the deal.** If the addendum shows paint protection at $799 and the deal jacket shows $999, you have a documentation problem. The two must be consistent.

**Outdated templates.** Using a template with products or pricing that's no longer current (because someone changed the pricing but didn't update the template) is a daily risk at dealerships without automated addendum systems.

## How This Works for Used Vehicles

Used vehicles don't have Monroney labels — those are a new vehicle requirement. But used vehicles have their own set of documentation requirements.

The FTC requires a **Buyers Guide** on every used vehicle offered for sale. Like the Monroney label for new cars, it must be displayed on the vehicle, and there are strict requirements about what it must contain (warranty status, covered systems, the airbag recall disclosure added in 2024).

A dealer addendum on a used vehicle typically shows:
- The asking price
- Any dealer-added products (tint, protection packages, GPS trackers)
- Reconditioning costs being passed to the buyer
- CPO certification details if applicable

## For Franchise Dealers: Keeping Both Consistent

Franchise dealers have a third layer of complexity: OEM requirements. Your dealer agreement may specify how dealer addendums must be formatted, what products you're authorized to offer, and how mark-ups must be disclosed.

Some OEM programs prohibit certain types of dealer mark-ups. Others require specific language about market adjustments. [Ford dealers](/lp/ford-dealers) and [Kia dealers](/lp/kia-dealers) work with their factory reps to understand their specific addendum guidelines — and their addendum software needs to support those requirements.

## The Practical Bottom Line

Every new vehicle needs: Monroney label (from the factory, don't remove it) + dealer addendum (from you, must be accurate and itemized).

Every used vehicle needs: FTC Buyers Guide (current 2024 version) + used vehicle sticker or addendum showing asking price and any dealer-added items.

Getting both documents right on every vehicle, every time, is easier said than done at high volume — especially when your product lineup, pricing, or staff changes frequently.
