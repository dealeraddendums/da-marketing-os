The FTC Used Car Rule has been federal law since 1985, but that doesn't mean every dealer is following it correctly. In fact, it's one of the most common compliance violations the FTC cites during dealer audits — and the penalties are real. Fines can reach $51,744 per violation.

If you're selling used vehicles, here's exactly what you need to know about Buyers Guide requirements in 2026.

## What Is the FTC Buyers Guide?

The Buyers Guide (also called the "window sticker" for used vehicles) is a federally required disclosure form that must be displayed on every used vehicle you offer for sale. It tells buyers:

- Whether the vehicle comes with a dealer warranty or is sold "as-is"
- What systems are covered under any dealer warranty and for how long
- What percentage of repair costs the dealer will pay
- A recommendation to get the vehicle inspected before purchase
- Information about obtaining a vehicle history report

The form must be displayed prominently on the vehicle — typically in the window — whenever the car is on your lot and available for sale.

## The 3 Types of Buyers Guides

### 1. As-Is — No Dealer Warranty

This is the most common form for independent used car dealers. It states clearly that the dealer offers no warranty and that the buyer assumes all responsibility for repairs after the sale.

To use this form legally, the dealer must not be providing any warranty at all — not even a verbal one. An informal "I'll take care of any issues" comment from a salesperson can void your As-Is status in court.

### 2. Implied Warranties Only

Used when state law prohibits As-Is sales for some or all vehicles. In states like Massachusetts, Minnesota, and Mississippi, dealers are required to provide at minimum an implied warranty of merchantability. The Buyers Guide must reflect this.

If you do business in multiple states, know your state-specific rules. What's legal in Texas may not be legal in Connecticut.

### 3. Full or Limited Dealer Warranty

If you're providing a warranty on the vehicle, this form specifies exactly what's covered (engine, transmission, electrical systems, etc.), the percentage the dealer pays, and the duration.

Every single covered system must be listed individually. Vague language like "powertrain" without specifying what's included doesn't meet the standard.

## What Changed: The 2024 FTC Amendments

The FTC updated the Used Car Rule in 2024. Key changes that affect your compliance:

**Airbag disclosure.** You must now disclose whether the vehicle has any unrepaired, open airbag safety recalls. This appears as a new check box on the revised Buyers Guide form. If a vehicle has an open airbag recall that hasn't been repaired, you must check "Yes" and inform the buyer.

**Updated formatting.** The revised form has a cleaner layout and updated language. The older pre-2024 form is no longer compliant for new sales.

**Spanish-language requirement.** If you make the sale in Spanish (or if your primary negotiation is in Spanish), you must provide the Buyers Guide in Spanish. This applies even if you operate in an English-dominant market but have Spanish-speaking staff who negotiate deals.

## Common Compliance Mistakes

**Not displaying it at all.** The Buyers Guide must be on the vehicle whenever it's offered for sale — not just when a customer asks about it.

**Using outdated forms.** Pre-2024 forms are no longer compliant. If your printer is still running the old template, you're exposed.

**Signature at the wrong time.** The buyer must sign and keep a copy of the Buyers Guide at the time of sale — not after they've already driven off the lot.

**Missing the airbag disclosure.** This is new and easy to miss. If you haven't updated your template to include it, every vehicle you've sold since 2024 without it is a potential violation.

**Warranty language doesn't match the form.** If your service contract says one thing and the Buyers Guide says another, you have a problem. The two documents must be consistent.
